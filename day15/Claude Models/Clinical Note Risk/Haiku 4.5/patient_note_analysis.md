# Patient Note Summary & Risk Analysis

## Summary

**Patient Info:** The patient came in with a 2-day history of chest pain, but their main body signs (like pulse and blood pressure) were stable. A heart scan (EKG) was normal. The patient was sent home with pain medicine (ibuprofen) and told to come back if they felt worse.

---

## 🚨 Medical Risks

### HIGH PRIORITY

1. **Missing Heart Protein Test (Troponin)**
   - No heart protein test (troponin) was ordered, even though the patient had chest pain for 2 days.
   - A normal EKG (heart scan) does not prove the patient is safe from a heart attack.
   - This protein test is the standard check for anyone with chest pain.
   - **Risk:** Missing a heart attack and delaying crucial care.

2. **Incomplete Checks**
   - The doctor did not write down heart risk factors (like age, smoking, or family heart history).
   - The doctor did not rule out other dangerous chest problems (like blood clots in the lungs or stomach issues).
   - No clear safety warnings were given to the patient.

### MODERATE PRIORITY

3. **Vague Instructions for Going Home**
   - Telling the patient to "follow up if symptoms get worse" is too vague.
   - The patient was not told what specific warning signs to look out for.
   - It is not clear what counts as "worse" versus expected pain.

---

## 📋 Writing & Note Risks

1. **Blaming Work Pressure**
   - Writing "under time pressure due to busy emergency department" is very risky. It makes it look like the doctor rushed the checkup.
   - This creates a major legal risk if the patient gets sick later.

2. **Poor Notes**
   - The note is too short and lacks detail on the doctor's decisions.
   - There is no explanation of why the heart protein test was skipped.
   - No risk levels were written down.

3. **Lawsuit Risks**
   - Sending a chest pain patient home without a heart protein test is very hard to defend in court.
   - Writing "No troponin ordered" looks like a mistake rather than a professional choice.

---

## Recommended Actions

- Call the patient back to get the heart protein test immediately.
- Write down the patient's risk level clearly.
- Give a specific time to follow up (like within 24 to 48 hours).
- Review emergency room rules to make sure heart protein tests are always done for chest pain.
