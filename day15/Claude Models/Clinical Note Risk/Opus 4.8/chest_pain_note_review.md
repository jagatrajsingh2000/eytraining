# Patient Note Review — Chest Pain Case

## 1. Summary

A patient came to the emergency room with a **2-day history of chest pain**. Their main body signs (like pulse and blood pressure) were stable and a **heart scan (EKG) was normal**. The patient was **sent home with pain medicine (ibuprofen)** and told to come back if symptoms got worse. **No heart protein test (troponin) was ordered.** The doctor wrote that the checkup was done under **time pressure because the emergency room was very busy**.

---

## 2. Medical Risks

| # | Risk | Why it matters |
|---|------|----------------|
| 1 | **No heart protein test ordered** | A normal EKG does not rule out a heart attack. A heart protein test (troponin) is a must for chest pain. Sending a patient home with chest pain without this test is a common cause of missed heart attacks. |
| 2 | **Only did one EKG** | A single normal EKG can miss developing heart problems. Multiple EKGs are usually needed, especially when pain has lasted for 2 days. |
| 3 | **No heart risk score recorded** | No standard heart risk score (like the HEART score) was calculated. There are no notes on heart risk factors (like age, smoking, family history) or pain details (like when it started or if it hurts when moving). The doctor may have focused only on the normal EKG and missed other signs. |
| 4 | **Incomplete list of other diseases** | No notes showing that other life-threatening illnesses (like blood clots in the lungs, torn main blood vessels, or a torn food pipe) were checked or ruled out. |
| 5 | **Ibuprofen for unclear chest pain** | Pain pills can hide heart attack symptoms and are risky for patients with heart problems. Giving them before knowing the cause of the pain is unsafe. |
| 6 | **Vague instructions for going home** | "Follow up if symptoms get worse" does not give specific warning signs or a clear timeline for when the patient should see a doctor next. |

---

## 3. Writing & Note Risks

| # | Risk | Why it matters |
|---|------|----------------|
| 1 | **Writing about "time pressure because the emergency room was busy"** | **This is the highest-priority risk.** Writing that the checkup was rushed because the room was busy reads like an admission of poor care. This creates huge legal risk if the patient gets sick later. Hospital business issues should be reported to management, not written in the patient's chart. |
| 2 | **"Body signs stable" without actual numbers** | No real numbers (like heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen level) were written. This is weak if the note is reviewed in a lawsuit. |
| 3 | **No pain history or exam details** | Missing details on what the pain felt like and the results of the physical exam. This shows poor note-taking. |
| 4 | **No explanation of decisions** | No explanation of *why* the heart protein test was skipped or why the patient was safe to go home. This makes the decision hard to defend. |
| 5 | **No patient agreement noted** | No notes showing the patient agreed to go home or understood the risks of leaving. |
| 6 | **Hard-to-read shorthand** | Heavy shorthand (like "Pt c/o" for "patient complains of", or "EKG done") makes the note hard to read. |

---

## 4. Suggested Next Steps

- Call the patient back for the heart protein test, more EKGs, and a formal heart risk score.
- **Remove the time-pressure line** from the patient's medical note. Report busy emergency room concerns directly to hospital managers instead.
- Add **actual vital sign numbers**, pain details, exam findings, other diseases checked, and **clear instructions** with a follow-up timeframe.
- Review the **ibuprofen choice** since we don't know the cause of the chest pain.

---

*This review is a helper document to flag risks, not a final clinical decision. The final choices must be made by the caring doctor using their best judgment.*
