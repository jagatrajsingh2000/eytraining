# Patient Note Summary & Risk Review

**Date of Review:** 2026-06-17
**Document Type:** Emergency Room Visit Note

---

## Clinical Summary

A patient came to the emergency room with a **2-day history of chest pain**. Body signs (like pulse and blood pressure) were noted as stable. A 12-lead heart scan (EKG) was performed and reported as normal. The patient was **sent home** with ibuprofen (a pain medicine) and told to come back if symptoms got worse. The doctor wrote that the room was very busy and there was high time pressure.

---

## 🔴 Medical Risks (High Priority)

### 1. Heart Protein Test (Troponin) Not Ordered
**Risk Level: HIGH**
No heart protein test (troponin) was done for a patient with 2 days of chest pain. Checking troponin levels is a key step to rule out heart attacks. A normal EKG does not prove there is no heart muscle damage — up to half of heart attack patients have a normal EKG at first.

> **Danger:** Missing a heart attack. This is a big safety risk for the patient.

### 2. Pain Pills (Ibuprofen) Given Before Knowing the Cause
**Risk Level: HIGH**
Ibuprofen is unsafe or risky for several chest pain causes:
- **Heart attack or clogged arteries** – Pain pills increase heart risks and can cause bad outcomes after a heart attack.
- **Heart swelling (inflammation)** – While ibuprofen is used here, aspirin is often preferred, and other pills are needed too.
- **Stomach sores (ulcers)** – Pain pills can make stomach pain worse.

Giving ibuprofen before knowing what is causing the chest pain was not safe.

### 3. No List of Other Sicknesses Written Down
**Risk Level: MEDIUM**
There is no note listing other possible causes for the chest pain. Serious conditions (like heart attacks, torn main blood vessels, or blood clots in the lungs) should be checked and written down as ruled out.

### 4. No Heart Risk Score Recorded
**Risk Level: MEDIUM**
No standard chest pain risk tool (like the HEART score) was used. This is standard care in emergency rooms and helps prove it was safe to send the patient home.

---

## 🟡 Note-Taking & Writing Risks (Medium Priority)

### 5. Blaming Time Pressure for Choices
**Risk Level: MEDIUM–HIGH**
The doctor wrote that the busy emergency room and time pressure affected the visit. While hospitals get busy, writing that pressure caused the doctor to skip a standard test (like the heart protein test) is a major legal risk. It will not protect the doctor in a lawsuit.

### 6. Missing Vital Sign Numbers
**Risk Level: LOW–MEDIUM**
Just writing "body signs stable" is not enough. Actual numbers for heart rate, blood pressure, breathing rate, oxygen level, and temperature must be written. "Stable" is just an opinion; real numbers are facts.

### 7. No Details About the Pain
**Risk Level: LOW–MEDIUM**
The note does not say what the chest pain felt like:
- Type of pain (sharp, heavy pressure, burning)
- If it spreads (to the arm, jaw, or back)
- Other symptoms (shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, passing out)
- If it started during rest or exercise
- Past heart history

### 8. No Specific Follow-Up Plan
**Risk Level: LOW**
"Follow up if symptoms get worse" is too vague. Best practice is to give a specific time (like "see your family doctor in 24–48 hours") and write down specific warning signs.

## Recommendations

1. **Immediate:** Call the patient back for a heart protein test and EKG check, especially if they still have pain.
2. **Short-term:** Check the ibuprofen prescription. Consider stopping it or replacing it with paracetamol (a milder pain reliever) until we know what is causing the pain.
3. **Emergency Room Rules:** Review emergency room rules to make sure heart protein tests are always done for chest pain, no matter how busy it gets.
4. **Notes:** Update the note with real vital numbers, other diseases ruled out, risk scores, and clear follow-up instructions.
